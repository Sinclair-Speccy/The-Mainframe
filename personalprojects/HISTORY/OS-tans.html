1 - OS-tans, their origins and the Nijiura community at Futaba

When you think of OS-tans you likely picture the “main cast” such as XP-tan, 95-tan, Me-tan, 2K-tan and so. Most fans simply admire these characters but after spending time in several OS-tan (or related) communities, I noticed common issues in how OS-tan information is presented, how research is conducted and even in the way their backstories are explained. These shortcomings motivated me to write this piece. Before I delve into those criticisms though it’s important to explain where OS-tans came from. While researching unrelated OS-tan topics, I found a blog that discussed some of these issues, many of my thoughts below are based on that blog, as well as my own experiences. (You’ll find a link to it at the end.)

So, what exactly are OS-tans? Technically, it is more accurately to call them “OS-musume” now (a point I’ll explain later) but they are best known moe personifications of operating systems that are primarily Windows, originating from Futaba’s Nijiura image board. While the term “OS-tan” is popular among English speakers, within the Futaba community the characters are usually called OS-musume. Although the concept of moe personification is not new, OS-tans have a particularly interesting origin story.

Nijiura, one of the boards on Futaba Channel, is known for its chaotic atmosphere and an unspoken rule regarding image usage. Members recognise that images posted there can be freely referenced, modified or incorporated into new stories. This collaborative spirit often leads to images being edited such as line drawings being coloured or refined in unexpected ways, which can transform simple sketches or collages into stories. This is why you might see many different versions of an OS-tan drawing (for instance, various Windows 95 wallpapers which can be seen on the old image vault).

Criticism of such modifications is rare; the community generally rejects changes that detract from the overall enjoyment. It’s this chaotic yet creative process that gave birth to OS-tans on Futaba’s Nijiura board. Alongside the initial creators, many individuals, often referred to collectively as “Toshiaki”, emerged as artists and craftsmen who produced collages, gifs, and more. Some were anonymous, others were doujinshi or commercial artists and some worked exclusively on Futaba. In this way, OS-tans were not created by a single individual but rather by a loosely organised collective effort.

A guiding principle in the community is “Enjoyable and Exciting” (E&E). Even a simple image, an original drawing, or a seemingly trivial comment can spark unexpected discussions and lead to the creation of new ideas. Many well-known topics within the community likely started as spontaneous posts that evolved into significant discussions which how OS-tans actually came to be. In the OS-tan fan book it says that Me-tan was made on August 6, 2003 when an image of the System Properties for Windows Me was posted alongside the comment “But Windows Me is cute even though it's no good.” This remark emphasised Windows Me’s endearing quality: cute yet clumsy, and lovable despite its flaws. Toshiaki and others agreed with this sentiment and by around 7 AM two “Me personifications” were quickly improvised, marking the start of the larger “Futaba OS Personification” movement. On Futaba Channel, any interesting material or idea whether from a chat, a reply, or an image is quickly transformed into collages, drawings, or joke replies. This spontaneous collaboration has led to significant trends, often referred to as “festivals.”
